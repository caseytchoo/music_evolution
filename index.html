Music's Loudness Over The Years

As technology and social moods change, so does the loudness of the music we listen to. We examined how music volume has evolved over the decades, speculated on the underlying reasons, and explored its relationships with acousticness and instrumentalness.

Music loudness rapidly increased starting in the 1990s—likely due to the rise of electronic music and digital mastering standards that reduced dynamic range in the “Loudness War.”

Loudness and acousticness show a moderate negative correlation, suggesting the shift toward electronic production reduced acoustic elements while increasing overall volume.

Instrumentalness shows almost no correlation with loudness—vocal presence does not drive perceived volume.

◆

Music and Emotions

Emotions are at the heart of music. This section explores how happiness, sadness, violence, and romanticism in songs have evolved over seven decades and their relationship with loudness.

Happiness remains the dominant emotion across all decades. Sadness and romanticism declined, while violence slightly increased. Happiness peaked in the 1960s–80s before gradually decreasing.

Reggae and hip-hop consistently convey the highest happiness, while jazz and rock remain the least happy. All genres show a decline in happiness from the 1950s to the 2010s.

Loudness vs Happiness & Sadness

High-density regions cluster at loudness 0.6–0.8 and happiness 0.5–0.8 → louder music tends to feel happier.

Loud music shows very weak association with sadness (most dense at sadness ≈ 0–0.2).

◆

Conclusion

Over the past seven decades, music has become significantly louder while happiness—despite remaining the dominant emotion—has gradually declined across all genres. The rise of electronic production appears to have simultaneously increased loudness and reduced acousticness. Louder songs today are more likely to evoke happiness than sadness, reflecting broader cultural and technological shifts in music production and consumption.
