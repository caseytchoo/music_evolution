HOW MUSIC’S SONIC, GENRES AND EMOTIONAL QUALITIES HAVE CHANGED OVER TIME

Casey Tchoo1, Minjun Park1and Alexander Queguiner1,
1George Mason University Korea

Abstract

Music evolves alongside culture, technology, and emotion. Over recent decades, production methods and genre preferences have changed dramatically, shaping how songs sound and feel. This research examines how genre dominance, valence, and loudness in popular music have evolved over time, revealing patterns that show how technology and culture have reshaped the modern soundscape. The Music Dataset (1950–2019) from Kaggle was used, containing approximately 28,000 songs described by 31 musical and lyrical variables. Each record includes information such as artist, track title, genre, and features like loudness, energy, danceability, valence, and acousticness. Data cleaning, processing, and visualization were performed in Python using Pandas for analysis and Matplotlib for visualization.

Music and Emotions

The emotional element is an essential part of music as music, and indeed any art form, is designed to evoke specific emotions in the listener/viewer. This section examines how the emotions contained in music have evolved over time. It also explores the relationship between loudness and emotion, as mentioned in the section above.

Heatmap

How have the four emotions expressed in songs—happiness, sadness, violence, and romantic—changed over time? Which emotion is most prevalent in songs?

This heatmap shows how happiness, sadness, violence, and romantic feelings, measured on a scale of 0 to 1, changed over time. Sadness and romanticism decreased, while violence increased. Happiness grew from 1960 to 1980, but has been declining since the 1990s. The changes in all emotional categories were not significant. Across all decades, happiness is consistently and overwhelmingly higher than the other emotions.

Happeness trend over seven decades by genre

Genre is one of the main categories that distinguishes songs. How much happiness do each of the seven major genres (rock, reggae, pop, jazz, hip-hop, country, and blues) convey?

This graph shows how happiness changes over time by genre. Happiness is measured on a scale of 0 to 1. Compared to the 1950s, happiness decreased across all genres in the 2010s. Data for reggae and hip-hop first appeared in the 1960s and 1980s, respectively, as these genres were not popular before then. Happiness was highest in reggae and hip-hop, and lowest in jazz and rock.

Loudness VS Happiness/Sadness

This project explored the variation in loudness of songs in the section above. What interactions exist between loudness and the two main emotions, happiness and sadness? These two graphs below are KDE (Kernel Density Estimation) graphs comparing loudness and happiness/sadness. The darker the color, the more data is clustered together.

Feature A

High-density regions are concentrated in the loudness range of approximately 0.6–0.8 and happiness range of approximately 0.5–0.8, indicating that loud music is associated with greater feelings of happiness.

Feature B

High-density regions are concentrated in the loudness range of approximately 0.6–0.8 and the sadness range of approximately 0–0.2. This suggests that loud music is weakly associated with sadness.
