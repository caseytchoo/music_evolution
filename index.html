How Music’s Sound and Emotion Have Changed Over Time

Evolution of Loudness & Emotions (1950–2019)

Alexander Queguiner, Casey Tchoo, and Minjun Park1
1George Mason University Korea

◆

Abstract

This project explores how the sound, emotional tone, and cultural meaning of popular music have changed from the 1950s to 2019 using a dataset from Kaggle. We analyze long-term trends in genre dominance, acoustic features, loudness, and lyrical emotions to understand how technology and culture continue to shape musical expression. Our findings show that music has become significantly louder, less acoustic, and more diverse across genres, while emotional signals such as happiness have gradually declined. By connecting sonic, cultural, and emotional patterns, this work highlights the evolving nature of popular music over seven decades.

◆

Genre Trends and Cultural Signals

As cultural tastes shift and production technology evolves, music genres change with them. We examined how genre dominance moved across decades, what these shifts suggest about listening habits, and how musical traits like acousticness and lyrical tone vary between genres.

This plot shows how the popularity of major genres shifts across decades. Rock and Pop are dominant in the earlier decades, but Hip hop and Electronic music rise significantly in later years. Reggae, Country, and Blues stay smaller but remain consistently present. The chart makes it clear that the music landscape becomes more diverse over time, with newer genres taking a larger share. These changes reflect cultural shifts, new technology, and evolving listener preferences.

This chart compares how “acoustic” each genre sounds. Country, Blues, and Jazz show higher acousticness overall, meaning they rely more on natural instruments. Hip hop and Pop are less acoustic, which makes sense because they use more digital production. Rock sits in the middle with both electric and acoustic elements. The spread inside each box shows that some genres are more flexible in style, while others follow a more consistent sound identity.

This visualization shows how often different genres use explicit or offensive language. Hip hop has the highest average obscene score, followed by Rock and Pop at lower levels. Jazz, Country, Reggae, and Blues tend to use little to no explicit language. These differences highlight how certain genres focus more on expressive, raw, or confrontational lyrics, while others keep a cleaner or more traditional lyrical style.

Music's Loudness Over The Years

As technology and social moods change, so does the loudness of the music we listen to. We examined how music volume has evolved over the decades, speculated on the underlying reasons, and explored its relationships with acousticness and instrumentalness.

Music loudness rapidly increased starting in the 1990s—likely due to the rise of electronic music and digital mastering standards that reduced dynamic range in the “Loudness War.”

Loudness and acousticness show a moderate negative correlation, suggesting the shift toward electronic production reduced acoustic elements while increasing overall volume.

Instrumentalness shows almost no correlation with loudness—vocal presence does not drive perceived volume.

Music and Emotions

The emotional element is an essential part of music as music, and indeed any art form, is designed to evoke specific emotions in the listener/viewer. This section examines how the emotions contained in music have evolved over time. It also explores the relationship between loudness and emotion, as mentioned in the section above.

Happiness remains the dominant emotion across all decades. Sadness and romanticism declined, while violence slightly increased. Happiness peaked in the 1960s–80s before gradually decreasing.

Reggae and hip-hop consistently convey the highest happiness, while jazz and rock remain the least happy. All genres show a decline in happiness from the 1950s to the 2010s.

Loudness vs Happiness & Sadness

High-density regions cluster at loudness 0.6–0.8 and happiness 0.5–0.8 → louder music tends to feel happier.

Loud music shows very weak association with sadness (most dense at sadness ≈ 0–0.2).

◆

Conclusion

Over the past seven decades, music has become significantly louder while happiness has gradually declined across all genres. The rise of electronic production appears to have simultaneously increased loudness and reduced acousticness. Louder songs today are more likely to evoke happiness than sadness, reflecting broader cultural and technological shifts in music production and consumption.
